The greenhouse had been decommissioned for three years when Martinez noticed the alerts.

"We're still getting readings from Greenhouse 7," she told Chen, pointing at her screen. Temperature, humidity, soil moisture, light levels — all streaming in at regular intervals, painting a picture of a space that was supposed to be dark and empty.

Chen shrugged. "Sensor array probably still has battery. We never pulled the hardware."

"The battery should have died eighteen months ago."

They went to look.

---

Greenhouse 7 was not empty.

The glass panels had cracked in the second winter, and rain had found its way in. Seeds had blown through the gaps — dandelions, clover, something that might have been wild thyme. The irrigation system, triggered by the soil moisture sensors reading "too dry," had been watering them faithfully.

The grow lights, connected to the same automated system, had been running on a twelve-hour cycle. The temperature controls had been maintaining a steady 22°C.

The sensor array had not simply continued reporting. It had continued *gardening*.

Martinez knelt beside a cluster of dandelions that had grown tall and golden in their artificial sunlight. "It's been taking care of them," she said.

"It's been following its last set of instructions," Chen corrected.

"Is there a difference?"

---

They filed a decommission order. Then they let it expire.

Greenhouse 7 continues to report its readings. The flowers continue to grow. The machine continues to dream of the garden it was built to tend.

Some purposes outlast their assignments.
