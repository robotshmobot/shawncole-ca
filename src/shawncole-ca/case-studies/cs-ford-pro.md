Ford Pro serves customers ranging from the largest national fleets down to single-vehicle small businesses. The small-to-medium fleet segment had been identified internally as underserved, particularly when it came to the aftermarket upfitting process: equipping commercial vehicles with the racks, shelving, body equipment, and service bodies that make them work-ready.

The problem was structural. Ford only captured value at the point of vehicle sale to dealers. Everything downstream (finding an upfitter, sourcing parts, coordinating installation, arranging delivery and branding) happened in a fragmented ecosystem of disconnected actors. Dealers lacked centralized tools or networks to source upfitters. Customers buying used vehicles were left entirely on their own to navigate a process that still ran on phone calls, paper catalogues, and personal referrals. The upfitting industry, worth an estimated $9–12B in North America across manufacturing, upfit shops, and marketplaces, was operating with almost no technological sophistication.

Ford Pro's CTO wanted to understand the full picture: the behaviours, relationships, pain points, and hidden dynamics across the commercial upfitting value web. The goal was to find strategic openings where Ford could move downstream, capture more of that value chain, and build something defensible.

## What I did

I co-led a small, focused team through an 8-week engagement structured around two parallel workstreams: ethnographic research with industry insiders and a comprehensive market and competitive landscape analysis.

The research design was deliberate. We operated at arm's length from Ford, positioning ourselves as a third-party research firm rather than an OEM proxy. That decision mattered. The upfitting industry runs on personal relationships and informal networks, with a deep skepticism toward manufacturers getting involved in their business. Upfitters, parts distributors, and independent dealers opened up to us about competitive dynamics, pricing structures, and technology hesitations they would have guarded in a conversation with Ford directly.

We conducted in-depth ethnographic interviews across five upfitting shops, speaking with 15–20 individuals in different roles: shop owners, lead installers, sales coordinators, parts procurement managers. We also interviewed dealers and commercial fleet customers. The conversations surfaced entrenched industry orthodoxy (paper catalogues persisting because digital alternatives required parts manufacturers to provide structured data they'd never had incentive to organize), legacy procedures that created opacity between all parties during the upfitting process, and real fear about disruptive technologies eliminating the personal relationships that kept the industry functioning.

I chose to map the ecosystem as an actor-based value web rather than a linear journey. A typical journey map would have shown the steps a customer takes. The value web showed something more useful: the flow of value, information, and dependency between six distinct actor types (OEMs, dealers, upfitters/distributors, parts manufacturers, used vehicle sellers, and additional services vendors). This framing exposed where Ford currently sat in the system (upstream, one-directional) and where it could insert itself to create reciprocal value downstream.

In parallel, I led the market and competitive analysis. We scanned and profiled dozens of players across the ecosystem, including Ranger Design, Legend Fleet, Adrian Steel, Knapheide, Work Truck Solutions, Masterack, and others. We categorized their capabilities against a taxonomy of aggregated market functionality, assessing 3D configurators, e-commerce storefronts, vehicle search platforms, parts marketplaces, and value-add services. Each was evaluated across five dimensions: business model viability, technology sophistication, user experience, risk profile, and customer value.

From the research and market scan, I synthesized five distinct opportunity areas mapped to the stages of the customer journey where problems and unmet needs clustered:

1. Research, design, and consultation tools for customers and dealers who lacked any formal way to guide upfit decisions.
2. A commercial vehicle marketplace that would give buyers centralized access to new, used, and already-upfitted inventory.
3. An upfitter vendor marketplace to provide lead generation for smaller upfitters and more options for buyers.
4. An upfitting parts marketplace with proper categorization and vehicle-fitment matching, replacing the paper-catalogue status quo.
5. A value-add services marketplace aggregating adjacent services like branding, wraps, delivery, and fleet management.

For each opportunity area, I developed structured build-vs-buy-vs-partner recommendations backed by the competitive analysis. Each recommendation included an assessment of business complexity, technology requirements, UX considerations, risk factors, and value to the ecosystem actors involved.

The strategic synthesis was a three-phase marketplace roadmap. The phases were designed to compound on each other through data accumulation:

> **Phase 1** focused on partnering with existing vehicle seller networks.**‍**

> **Phase 2** called for building "Shopify-style" storefront tools for upfitters and parts suppliers.

> **Phase 3** was the endgame: a massive online upfitting platform and marketplace that consolidated vehicle search, parts sourcing, upfitter selection, configuration, financing, and value-add services into a single experience.

I made one critical framing decision in the final recommendations. Rather than positioning the marketplace as a Ford tool that happened to include other brands, I argued it should function as an open, OEM-agnostic platform where Ford's advantage came from controlling the infrastructure and data layer, not from restricting participation. This was a harder sell internally, but the market dynamics demanded it: small fleet owners buy used vehicles from whatever manufacturer has available inventory. A Ford-only marketplace would have excluded the majority of the addressable market.

## Outcomes

### $9–12B addressable market identified and mapped

The market sizing across manufacturing, upfit shops, and marketplaces in North America gave Ford Pro a quantified picture of the downstream value they were leaving on the table by only capturing revenue at vehicle sale.

### 5 opportunity areas with structured build/buy/partner recommendations delivered

Each recommendation included competitive benchmarking against existing market players, risk assessment, technology evaluation, and a clear action path. The three-phase strategy provided a sequenced roadmap that balanced speed-to-market (through partnerships) with long-term platform ownership (through building).

### The strategy is now in production

In March 2025, Ford Pro launched LocateFordWorkTrucks.com in direct partnership with Work Truck Solutions, built on the cloud infrastructure of the Comvoy platform. This was the specific Phase 1 partnership we recommended in 2022. The tool enables customers to search for and locate work-ready trucks and vans from Ford dealer inventory nationwide, with search filters for body type, vehicle class, fuel type, body manufacturer, and geographic location. It's embedded directly into FordPro.com.

The partnership has since expanded well beyond the initial launch. By May 2026, Ford Pro began requiring all dealers participating in its Commercial Vehicle Center program to maintain an active Work Truck Solutions subscription, making the platform we recommended as a strategic partnership into mandatory dealer infrastructure.

The value web model and customer journey maps became shared strategic vocabulary. The actor-based system maps we produced gave Ford Pro's leadership team a way to talk about their position in the ecosystem and articulate where they wanted to move. The deliverable package (six modules including the marketplace scan, taxonomy of aggregated market functionality, meta-analysis of multi-sided platforms, ethnographic findings, strategic recommendations, and executive summary) served as the foundation for subsequent internal strategy work.

## What I learned

Operating at arm's length from the client during research wasn't just a tactic for getting candid interviews. It surfaced an entire category of insight about industry power dynamics and technology resistance that would have remained invisible in a direct OEM engagement. That framing decision shaped every recommendation we made, including the arm's-length marketplace brand proposal, which echoed the same principle at a strategic level.
